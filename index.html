Mumps cases in central Ohio

The first case of mumps detected in Franklin County was January 7, with the first case at OSU detected February 10.1

At Ohio State University, the virus has affected 109 students, 17 staff members, two family members, and 25 with strong ties to the university community, with most affected OSU-affiliated persons living in Columbus. 95 females and 58 males are affected at OSU, from three to 58 years of age. The university-linked cases include residents in Columbus, Athens, Belmont, Delaware, Fairfield, Franklin, Hamilton, Licking, and Pickaway counties.2

Three to four cases have been reported at Columbus State Community College.3

Two cases have been reported in Olentangy Local Schools, leading school officials to tell students without the vaccine and with compromised immune systems to stay home. 4

Local patients have had the following complications:

  • Testicular inflammation
  • Ovarian inflammation
  • Deafness
  • Hospitalization

Frequently Asked Questions

Am I sick?

Symptoms of the mumps are similar to the flu and include a fever, headache, muscle aches, tiredness, swollen glands under the ears or jaw, and a loss of appetite. Symptoms may last seven to 10 days.

If you suspect that you are sick, visit a doctor. They will provide you with better information than random pages on the Internet.

The mumps virus spreads through saliva and through aerosols produced by sneezing or coughing. Wash your hands, and sneeze into your shoulder or elbow - not your hands. The virus takes 22 days to incubate and infect your body.

Should I get vaccinated?

If you have not already received both doses of the measles-mumps-rubella (MMR) vaccine, you should get vaccinated as soon as possible. If you have received only one dose, you should get vaccinated as soon as possible.

The MMR vaccine can be obtained at your family doctor, pharmacy clinics, university student health services (OSU), and Columbus Public Health walk-in clinics. The CPH walk-in clinic is held at 240 Parsons Avenue, Columbus, Ohio at the following times:

  • Mondays 8 a.m. - 5 p.m.
  • Tuesdays 10 a.m. - 7 p.m.
  • Wednesdays 8 a.m. - noon
  • Thursdays 8 a.m. - 5 p.m.
  • Fridays 8 a.m. - 5 p.m.

Are vaccines harmful?

No. Just -- no.

Unvaccinated people transmit dangerous diseases. Mumps can lead to deafness and infertility. Vaccination protects you and your children against that risk. The thiomersal mercury preservative was removed from vaccines in 2001. Andrew Wakefield's research and paper alleging a connection between the MMR vaccine and autism were unethical, fraudulent, irresponsible, falsified and abusive.

Vaccines save lives.
